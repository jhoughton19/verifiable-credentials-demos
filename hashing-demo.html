Tampering simulated One byte of the document was modified — a single character changed. Compare the verification hash to the recorded hash: nearly every character of the 64-character digest is different. This is the avalanche property of SHA-256, and it is why any alteration is detectable.

What this demonstration proves

  1. Determinism. The same document always produces the same 256-bit digest. Anyone with the document can independently verify the hash — no trusted database required.
  2. Tamper-evidence. Altering a single byte changes the digest almost entirely (the avalanche property). Any post-recording alteration is detectable by anyone who has the recorded hash.
  3. Privacy by computation. The hash reveals nothing about the document's contents. A county recorder can publish a hash without disclosing the underlying record.
  4. Statutory fit. SHA-256, applied at the moment of recording and re-verified on access, satisfies the "tamper-evident technology" requirement of Utah Code § 46-1-2(24) and the immutability characteristic required by § 63A-12-117(4)(b)(i).
